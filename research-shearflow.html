Shear flow in partially vegetated open channel

A numerical model for shear flow in the vegetated wetlands. The model solves shallow water equations with Finite Volume Method, which is characterized by using HLLC approximate Riemann solver and MUSCL-Hancock time integration. The model is applied to the laboratory study on shear flow in a partially vegetated open channel by White and Nepf (2007).

Introduction

Shear flows, in general, are formed across an interface between two layers with distinctive hydrodynamic drags. Such flows are often characterized by the formation of coherent turbulent structures. The existence of coherent turbulent structures has profound effects on the exchange of mass and momentum between the two layers. Shear flows are ubiquitous in many aquatic systems that exhibit an interface between a high conveyance channel and dense vegetation, such as tidal creeks with fringing mangroves, rivers with floodplains, salt marches etc. The material exchange due to shear flow is important for the hydrologic, morphologic, and ecological processes in these systems. The objective is to develop a numerical model to simulate shear flow but focus on reproducing the laboratory shear flow in a partially vegetated channel conducted by Nepf's group at MIT. Please fork Github (coming soon) for the source code of the model, which is general application of shallow water equations, not limited to this study.

Methodology

As in White and Nepf (2007), shallow water condition is considered with hydrostatic pressure assumption, which yields the standard shallow water equations. The drag is parameterized the same way as White and Nepf (2007) to account for the difference drag forces between two flow regions, i.e. the drag forces due to friction over the river floor alone and the drag force preserving the dynamic effect of the vertical shear due to the porous layer .

Shear Fields

Mass conservation with HLLC Rieman solver for the flux to reduce numerical diffusion

Second Order Accuracy

2nd order spatial reconstruction with MUSCL-Hancock time integration

Non-Reflective BC

Neuman boundary condition at the downstream to allow free flow condition

Model Setup

The numerical simulation is set up the same way as the experiments in White and Nepf (2007). The experiment was carried out in a 120 cm wide, 1300 cm long channel with 40 cm wide vegetation canopy on one side. The Channel was discretized by non-overlapping rectangular cells with water depth of 6.6 cm and bottom friction of 0.010. The resistance parameter due to vegetation within the canopy is 9.2 cm-1.

Development

As the flow develops, the momentum thickness initially growths, and reaches an equilibrium width at 600 cm once the flow is fully established, because the vegetation drag limits momentum penetration into the canopy. Correspondingly, energy is shifted to lower modes, and concentrates at a single dominant frequency of 0.032 as indicated by the power spectral density of the transverse velocity fluctuations just above the interface, which agrees with the prediction by Ho and Huerre (1984).

Equilibrium

The equilibrium mean velocity profile is characterized by two layer structure with outer layer thickness and inner layer thickness separated by an inflect point. The inflect point is at the interface of the canopy, which is coincident with the peak of the equilibrium Reynolds stress. The coherent fluctuations are indicative of instability of the Kelvin-Helmholtz type, which is characterized by coherent vortex structures. The primary vortex is defined as the region between two saddle points, which is shown for velocity and the corresponding streamline with the spatial coordinates normalized by the outer layer’s thickness. The vortex resides primarily in the open flow region, with a length of approximately 10 times the outer layer thickness, which agrees well with the laboratory study.
