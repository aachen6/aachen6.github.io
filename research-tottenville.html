Design of Shore Protection for Tottenville

This project is to design a mile long shore protection structures with the objective to reduce wave action and coastal erosion while enhancing ecosystems and beach access along the shoreline of Tottenville, NY. The design includes five unique segments, i.e. earthen berm, wetland eco-revetment, hybrid sand dune, eco-revetment, and raised edge pathway. The overall project objective and design processes with the corresponding engineering analysis are presented here with special focus on aspects of storm resiliency and 2-D phase-resolving wave modelling .

Introduction

The Tottenville Shoreline Protection Project (TSPP) in Staten Island, is managed through the contract with the Dormitory Authority of the State of New York (DASNY) with the end client being New York State Governor’s Office of Storm Recovery (GOSR) and the NYC Parks Department. The goal of this project is to reduce wave action and coastal erosion along the shoreline in Tottenville while also enhancing ecosystems and shoreline access and use.

Coastal Resiliency

The coast is a dynamic environment that responds to coastal processes and hazards from the everyday to the major event. To address the TSPP project goals, each of the coastal processes and hazards illustrated and described here are taken into account through the modeling and design process. The design mainly addresses processes such as waves and erosion with design and construction of countermeasures that mitigate or reduce their impacts. Processes like sea level rise and storm surge act on scales greater than our control, the effects from which are incorporated in the analysis. In short, the TSPP project designs to a 25-year event with IPCC 2050 sea level rise and incorporates stability for loads associated with a 100-year event for a 50-year design life.

Ecological Enhancement

The second objective of the TSPP project is to enhance ecosystems and shoreline access and use, which are achieved through dune habitat enhancement, wetland enhancement, and shoreline planting.

Design Elements

These goals will be achieved using a layered approach consisting of

  • Earthen Berm: A raised berm constructed of soil and earth material and planted
  • Wetland Eco-Revetment: Concrete block with pathway looking over the existing wetland area
  • Hybrid Dune: A sand dune with interior armor core revetment placed below the dune sand and planted with dune grasses
  • Eco-Revetment: Concrete block with steps and planters integrated with the existing road along the shore
  • Raised Edge: A pathway with stone revetment along the water front
  • Two Transition Nodes to provide access and transitions between the hybrid dune, eco-revetment, and raised edge.

STORM RESILIENCY

The proposed shoreline structures are being designed to reduce erosion and wave action, which raises a concern that the elevated shoreline may increase frequency, duration, or magnitude of inland flooding that would otherwise be flowing freely to the ocean. The objective of this task is to determine the potential impacts of the project on adjacent inland coastal areas and the resiliency of NYCDEP storm sewer systems located within the project limits. Among the hydrologic processes affecting the sewer system such as the rainfall runoff, the coastal inundation, and seepage/infiltration, one challenge is to determine the representative storm surge hydro-graph. This is not well studied especially for extra-tropical storms that affect the area the most and may have larger impact on the sewer system due to its long duration.

Although the peak water level (tide plus surge), the variable that is normally the focus, is an important design criteria, which is however not a true measurement of the storm strength. Instead, non-tidal residual and duration are two important aspects that are often overlooked. Non-tidal residual is the direct effect from the meteorological conditions associated with the storm system. Storm duration, particular continuous duration above the MHW, can cause sustained flooding as sewer system does not normally function above the MHW. The goal is to generate a synthetic hydro-graph with the maximum continuous duration above the MHW. Historical storms were identified through peak over threshold analysis and examined to address this issue. As the figure suggests, there is weak correlation among those parameters. Due to the time scale of tide and extra-tropical storm, storms with long duration above the MHW favors when storm occurs at neap tide with peak surge being coincident with low tide, which are referred to as surge dominated water level. Also, the hydro-graph shape is controlled not only by the peak water level of the desired return period but also the non-tidal residual with the equivalent return period. The storm hydro-graph is determined considering all those three variables.

FUNWAVE

The wave risk reduction assessment will be analyzed by a 2-D phase-resolving wave model, i.e. FUNWAVE, which is a Boussinesq model for simulating nearshore surface waves and currents. This section highlights some preliminary results. Presented here includes an instantaneous snapshot of water surface showing the details of the actual wave fields being captured by the model and an animation of wave transformations, runup and overtopping along a specific transect.
