Morphodynamic Impact Assessment of Remediation Design

Our client initiated remediation efforts for a Superfund site in lower Hudson River, including dredging of contaminated areas, design of a sloped shoreline and extended bulkhead alignment. We were asked to evaluate the impact on the local morphological change due to the remediation design. Aaron served as technical lead and led the numerical modeling efforts, including development and validation of hydrodynamic model for the lower Hudson River using Delft3D. He also led the development of a nested sediment and morphological model at the study area. Aaron used the model to assess the morphological impact due to the remediation design.

Introduction

The client (confidential for this presentation) owns a Superfund site in the lower Hudson River and has been initiating remediation efforts. Proposed remediation efforts which extend into the Hudson River include sediment dredging and backfill (no net fill), isolation capping (no net fill), installation of a containment bulkhead with backfill behind that bulkhead. Relevant remediation efforts adjacent to the river include excavation and backfill, installation of a sloped shoreline, and construction of a compensatory habitat mitigation feature. We were commissioned to assess the hydrodynamic and morphodynamic responses to proposed post-remediation conditions. A coarse resolution regional hydrodynamic model of the Lower Hudson River (LHR Model) and a fine resolution local hydrodynamic model focused at and around the site (HOH Model) were developed using Delft3D for this task.

Hydrodynamic

The LHR hydrodynamic model extends from the Battery upriver to Green Island, which is driven by an hourly time series of water levels at the Battery as the downstream boundary condition and daily time series of discharge at the Green Island as the upstream boundary condition. The hydrodynamics of the LHR model were validated against field observations at several stations along the Hudson River for select time periods in 2005 and 2011 as shown here.

Morphodynamic

The HOH morphodynamic model provides greater resolution in the project area, including but not limited to the proposed bulkhead and remediation dredging areas. The mesh captures those design features at a minimal resolution of 10 meters. The HOH model is driven by times series of water levels and velocities at the downstream and upstream boundaries, respectively, which are obtained from the regional LHR model.

Suspended Sediment Concentration

According to Ralston and Geyer (2009), suspended sediment concentration (SSC) present monthly trend and seasonal trend. The monthly trend of the SSC is characterized by a lower peak and higher peak,corresponding to neap and spring tide, respectively. The seasonal trend is characterized by a parabolic shape with lower SSC during summer and higher SSC in Spring and late Fall, which corresponds to the seasonal variation of upstream freshwater discharge. As shown in figure, the HOH model captured both the monthly and seasonal variations of SSC, as well as its magnitude.

Sedimentary Environment

Figure shows erosion and deposition pattern after one year simulation under existing conditions. The HOH morpho-dynamic model predicts deposition along the river bank to a distance of about 400 ft from the shoreline with areas farther from the shoreline experiencing erosion. This is in general quantitatively consistent with the sedimentary environment observed in the field as presented by Nitsche et al. (2007). Particularly, the transition areas from erosion to deposition (as indicated by light colors) match well with the boundary (solid blue line) between erosion and deposition zone presented in Nitsche et al. (2007). Ralston et al. (2012) also showed that in the lower Hudson River estuary, the channel is generally erosional, and the shallow areas along the river bank are depositional.

Design Versus Existing Condition

Figure shows the difference in erosion and deposition patterns under the existing and design conditions. The change in erosion and deposition patterns were generally limited to directly south and north of the proposed bulkhead, where shear stresses are reduced due to the physical obtrusion of the proposed bulkhead which reduces the flow downstream and upstream (i.e. north during flood tide and south during ebb tide). There is a slight increase of erosion or decrease of deposition just in front of the bulkhead to the west due to slightly contracted flow caused by the bulkhead. Some changes along the sloped shoreline and boat slips were also observed, which correspond to the planned changes in bathymetry in these areas under design conditions, i.e., deposits will be removed in these areas, lowering the elevation.
