Storm Surge and Wave Modeling for Lake Michigan

This project is to hindcast historical storms that have occurred in Lake Michigan for FEMA Region V, the results from which were used to drive the 1-D transect analysis for the determination of nearshore wave hazards beyond the surfzone limit. This page presents the development of the 2-D storm surge and wave modeling for Lake Michigan using coupled ADCIRC and SWAN, as well as the sensitivity analyses performed for the model development and its validation. The model is driven by lakewide wind and pressure fields associated with the historical storms and includes the effect from ice coverage over the lake for winter storms. Production model runs for 150 historical storms identified through gauge analysis are also presented.

Introduction

Prior to the modeling effort described herein, an independent technical review of recent coastal flood studies in the Great Lakes revealed technical deficiencies in those studies. Most paramount of these is the fact that starting wave conditions for 1-D transect analysis were taken from deep water, which fails to capture the more complex nature of wave refraction/diffraction that 2-D wave models can capture. Further, this method omits the development of the directional wave spectra in the nearshore region. To address this concern, a 2-D storm surge and wave model is developed using ADCIRC+SWAN to hindcast historical storms that have occurred in Lake Michigan. The model mesh covers the entirety of Lake Michigan and Lake Huron with grid resolution varying between 10 km in the center of Lake Huron to 15 m at the most detailed locations along the shoreline of Lake Michigan. It is necessary to include Lake Huron in the model domain in order to allow the two lakes to exchange flow through the Straits of Mackinac, which is critical to accurately simulate storm surge in Lake Michigan. The final mesh contains 1,298,483 elements and 667,396 nodes. The model is forced by hindcast spatially-varying wind and atmospheric pressure fields from CSFR. Ice coverage for winter storms was considered as well.

Sensitivity

Prior to validation, sensitivity analyses were performed on key model parameters for both ADCIRC and SWAN. These parameters represented a combination of both physical and numerical properties. The primary purpose of these sensitivity analyses was to produce accurate results while balancing the computational expense of the model. The sensitivity analyses were performed by converging analysis for parameters that are commonly studied such as

Offshore and Nearshore Resolution

Offshore resolution of 1000 m for wave generation with refinement to 500-700 m in areas with large gradients in bathymetry; nearshore resolution of 60 m with refinements to 15-30 m to resolve coastal structures.

Frequency/Directional Resolution

Frequency resolution was determined by SWAN automatically according to the requirement of df/f=0.01; Directional resolution of 5° was used to resolve swells propagating along the latitude-axis of Lake Michigan.

Coupling Time Step and Max Iteration

Sensitivity analyses on parameters that are often ignored, particularly, coupling time step between ADCIRC and SWAN and maximum iteration number, were also explored. Figure shows convergence rate and the number of iterations it takes for different combination of coupling time step and maximum iteration. Once the storm is fully developed with wind conditions being more consistent, it only takes 4 or 5 iterations for full convergence except for coupling time step being greater than 30 min. 15 min with maximum 5 iterations were selected in production runs weighting accuracy and numerical efficiency.

Validation

The model was validated for both water levels and waves using a total of six historical storm events. These events represent a variety of conditions and forcing mechanisms for typical types of storms occurred in the Great Lake with and without ice coverage, i.e. low pressure system with monotonic hydro-graph; event with seiche induced oscillations; and fast moving, short duration frontal passage storms. The validation subsets provided a comprehensive sampling of the overall storm event population. The validation exercise demonstrates that water levels are modeled to a reasonable accuracy in comparison to measured values. The model performs less well for fast moving squalls and within embayments when seiches are observed, which can be attributed to the sparse coverage of meteorological data from which meteorological forcing was developed.

1992 Storm

Low Pressure System with Single Peak

This event is a typical storm resulting from meso-scale meteorological conditions passing through Lake Michigan with super-imposed sub-scale local events. It best depicts the impact of the inverted barometer effect on peak water levels. The surge generated during this event spans approximately two days and was characterized by a monotonic hydro-graph without significant water level fluctuations prior to or after the event. The simulated water level at all stations compare well with the corresponding observations.

1990 Storm

Low Pressure System with Seiche

This winter event with ice coverage produced the highest recorded water level in the lake at the Green Bay gauge due to the presence of seiche. The storm system tracked from southwest to northeast with the storm center passing directly over the southern tip of Lake Michigan. The model well captured the peak surge, as well the seiche induced major oscillating modes after the peak.

1998 Storm

Fast moving fronts

The event occurred on May 31, 1998 during which a rapidly moving low pressure center propagated across the northern portion of Lake Michigan along a line from the southern end of Green Bay to Grand Traverse Bay. The rapid fluctuation in water level record at Calumet is a clear indication of the rapid speed associated with this weather system, which is not well captured by the model due to poor resolution of the CSFR meteorological data.

Production

For the purposes to obtain the 1-percent-annual-chance water levels and wave induced hazards, 150 events from 1960-2009 that produced the highest surge values and/or greatest waves were identified from gauge analysis and were hindcasted by the model. As the ultimate purpose of this effort to determine 1-percent-annual-chance event and the associated flood hazard,  simulated peak water levels from all storms were ranked and plotted together with that from the measurement at all NOAA gauges with long term water level records. The RMS error of the simulated water level is generally less than 0.1 m. Furthermore, 1-percent-annual-chance water levels at each gauge were analyzed using both measured and simulated water level by fitting the GEV distribution, which also shows a difference within 0.1 m.
