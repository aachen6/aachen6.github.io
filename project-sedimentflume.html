Tug Operation Induced Sediment Plume

The client is to construct transmission towers across a river with oyster lease farms. The objective of this project is to determine the sediment plume induced by tug operation during the construction period and its impact on oyster yields. The tug operation induced erosion and the subsequent sediment plume were analyzed.

Introduction

The client (confidential for this presentation) is the owner of electrical transmission power lines and associated towers and hardware that cross four private oyster leases and is adjacent to several others within the river studied. These electrical infrastructures are nearing the end of their design service life. As such, the client has proposed a project to replace the five existing support towers within the river as well as one terrestrial tower on either bank of the river. The existing lease agreement with these oyster leaseholders requires that the client notify them in advance of any construction. The client has notified the leaseholders and they are in negotiation regarding the construction of new towers within the lease areas. As part of this correspondence, concerns have been raised regarding the potential impact to oyster beds associated with construction activity, in particular, propeller-induced erosion/scour and suspension of bottom sediment due to tug boat and barge operations. We developed an approach that estimates bed shear stresses and the induced sediment suspension owing to propeller turbulence and barge wake. The subsequent sediment plume due to tidal flow and dispersion and its deposition were studied using a general morphological model.

Tug_Erosion_Model

Vessel movements generate the thrust, a force exerting on the water column by the propeller and its movement (prop wash and wake). As such, currents are generated, which may suspend sediments from the bed into the water column given the currents are strong enough to overcome the stress required for sediment erosion. Subsequently, suspended sediments will be transported by the currents therefore form sediment plume and deposit elsewhere. The overall approach to determine the vessel induced erosion includes the determination of thrust, velocity or stress fields associated with the prop wash and vessel wake, and erosion due to shear fields. The sediment flume can be modeled by a typical hydro- and morpho-dynamic model.

Shear Fields

The maximum bottom velocity (and shear stresses) due to prop wash and wake is calculated using the model developed by Maynord (2000). The model calculates the bottom velocities resulting from vessel movement and propeller action at specific locations relative to the propeller position. Since many marine vessels have multiple engines and propellers, the model also accounts for the interaction of the individual velocity fields generated by each propeller. Figure shows an example of the bottom shear stress in the area behind the tug’s propeller for a representative condition at the site.

Thrust

One major factor for the determination of shear fields is the thrust. The thrust is determined following the approach described in Hammack et al. (2008), which depends on vessel power and draft, propeller type and dimension.

Erosion Curves

The erosion rate can be determined using critical shear stress method, which states that the incipient sediment motion occurs as the bed shear stress is stronger than the critical shear stress and increases with increasing shear stress. The initial depth at each tower location ultimately governs the scour depth and mass flux, which is decreasing in time due to increase of water depth. The time series of scour depth (in meter) and sediment mass fluxes (in kg/s) at a particular tower location are presented here as an example.

Flume_Model

Tug will be operating locally to each tower and suspended sediment generated by the vessel is typically within tens of meters behind the vessel, therefore is treated as a point source. The resulting sediment plume and its deposition within the river are modeled using CurWaC2D-Sed, a 2-D depth-averaged morphodynamic model I developed as listed in the software section. The hydrodynamic part of the model is driven by discharge from the upstream and water level condition at the downstream. As a brief summary, the model is characterized by

Unstructured Mesh

Triangular mesh to resolve complex geometry and bottom topography with flexible mesh resolution

Semi-Implicit Eulerian-Lagrangian Method

Lagrangian treatment of the convection term to minimize numerical diffusion; and unconditionally stable scheme even with large time step through semi-implicit method for the hydro-static pressure to enhance model efficiency

Wetting and Drying

Moving boundary condition to capture flooding and drying in tidal flat areas

Results

The maximum suspended sediment concentration (SSC) and its deposition for one particular construction scenario during ebb tide are presented here. The highest SSC centers around the tower at construction and is transported downstream with reducing SSC value, most of which deposit in shallow areas along the banks. The accumulated deposition can be estimated, which provided the critical information, together with the maximum SSC from all construction scenarios, to evaluate the impact on the oyster yields.
