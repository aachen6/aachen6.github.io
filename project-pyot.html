Introduction

As coastal engineers, we are consistently dealing with wave-structure interaction processes such as wave reflection, wave transmission, and wave overtopping. Wave reflection in harbors can cause resonances, which enhance wave energy and put the coastal structures at risk.Wave transmission determines how much wave energy can penetrate through breakwaters therefore controls the wave energy approaching the shoreline. Wave overtopping poses directly thread to the properties behind coastal defenses. Accurate estimation of those processes is critical for the design of coastal and harbor structures and the assessment of their hydraulic performances.

In the literature, several empirical formulae are available for the prediction of the key parameters representing the wave-structure interaction. It is worthy to mention Van der Meer et al. (2005) for prediction of the wave transmission coefficient, Zanuttigh and van der Meer (2006) for the wave reflection coefficient, and EurOtop (2018) for the prediction of the wave overtopping discharge. Although the empirical formulae work well for specific structure types, it often fails to provide a complete description of different type of coastal structures, especially for structures with complex geometry. These parameters can also be estimated with complex computer models that are often expensive to develop and tend to over-estimate.On the latest advancement of this topic, a large dataset regarding wave reflection, transmission and overtopping has been collected through two EU projects DELOS and CLASH, which are subsequently merged with additional datasets as a homogeneous database by Zanuttigh and Van der Meer (2014). The final dataset includes 13,000 tests on wave overtopping, more than 7,000 tests on wave reflection and 3,500 tests on wave transmission, for a total amount of almost 18,000 tests. The development of this dataset made it possible to apply machine learning algorithms for the prediction of those parameters. The estimation of wave transmission coefficient and wave overtopping discharge were made using the Artificial Neural Network (ANN), a machine learning algorithm, during the DELOS and CLASH projects, respectively. Through a series of studies using the ANN, Zanuttigh and Van der Meer subsequently extended the application to the estimation of wave reflection coefficient and improved the predictions of wave transmission coefficient and wave overtopping from the early attempts.

ANN is a very basic technique in machine learning, where the techniques have been evolving rapidly. This advancement made machine learning a huge success in many applications including image recognition such as hand writing, medical diagnosis from X-Ray images, automated driving, and even generation of artificial arts etc., voice recognition such as virtual assistance, fraud prevention in email spanning and financial services,social media and advertising, translations among many others. The discussion of machine learning cannot be made without mentioning of Kaggle, which is on online community of data scientists and machine learners, owned by Google. Kaggle allows users to find and publish datasets, explore and build models in a web-based data-science environment, work with other data scientists and machine learning engineers, and enter competitions to solve data science challenges. Among the latest machine learning techniques, XGBoost has recently been dominating machine learning and Kaggle competitions. It was used in 17 winning solutions among the 29 challenges published at Kaggle in 2015. XGBoost stands for eXtreme Gradient Boosting,which is an algorithm for structured or tabular data. The idea is to apply the XGBoost algorithm for the estimation of the key parameters related to wave-structure interaction using the dataset described earlier. We aim to develop a model for accurate prediction of wave reflection and transmission coefficients and wave overtopping discharge, which will enable coastal engineers to design better coastal and harbor structures.
