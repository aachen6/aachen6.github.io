Introduction

The Grays Harbor Conservation District initiated a three-phased process in 2015 to investigate the deterioration problem of the shellfish aquaculture in Grays Harbor and Willapa Bay (Twin Harbors), WA. The deterioration is thought to be resulting from excessive sediment movement due to geomorphological changes associated with anthropogenic activities and from biological processes such as over population of the burrowing shrimps. We were scoped to investigate this problem with a comprehensive numerical model. Four data investigation and analysis were carried out simultaneously. A hydrodynamic and sediment transport model was developed and calibration using Delft3D-FM. The model performance to reproduce water level, current, and wave was evaluated graphically using Taylor’s Diagram, where the model skill well exceeds the industrial standard. The model also performs well to reproduce the morphological change at the inlets as compared with survey data as well as over tidal flats as compared with the Differential Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (DInSAR) data. The calibrated model was then used to understand the hydro- and morpho-dynamics within the Twin Harbors, to evaluate the impact from the USACE O&M dredging within Grays Harbor, and to define/evaluate mitigation measures offset impacts to shellfish growing beds in the Twin Harbors.

Data investigation

Four data investigation and analysis were carried out simultaneously, including online data search, generation of recent annual rate of the morphological changes over massive tidal flats using historical DInSAR, stakeholder survey using an interactive web-based GIS map, and USACE literature review. A comprehensive metocean data analysis was performed to understand seasonal and long-term variations of tide, current, wind, and wave, which are briefly discussed here.

Tide

Tides in the Twin Harbors are typical of the Pacific Coast of North America, which are mixed tide and exhibit diurnal inequality with two unequal high and low elevations each lunar day. The diurnal range is 9.14 ft at the harbor entrance, increasing to 10.11 ft at Aberdeen with a 1-hour phase lag because of the amplification of the tidal wave through the pear shape of the harbor. The relatively large tidal range along with the broad bay area leads to a significant volume of tidal exchange within the Grays Harbor with a Diurnal tidal prism volumes of approximately 1.7 x 1010 ft3.

Discharge

The seasonal variations of the riverine discharges are shown as violin plots. It’s clear that the seasonal variations of the discharge are very similar between the watersheds. River discharge is very small with confined distribution from May through October, and river discharge generally increases with large variations from November to March. The rainy seasons begin early in October and the annual spring snowmelt from the mountainous areas that feed into the rivers commonly occurs in February and March.

Wind

Winds over the Northeast Pacific generally correspond to the seasonal atmospheric cycles largely determined by  the movement of the North Pacific high-pressure system and the Aleutian low-pressure system in the Bering Sea. During the summer months, the East Pacific high-pressure system migrates northward from the equatorial region and becomes seasonally stationary off the coasts of California and Oregon and reaches its greatest development in July. This weather system generates clockwise winds with predominantly northwest and north winds.

During the winter months, the East Pacific high-pressure system retreats to the equatorial region and the Aleutian Low strengthen and migrate west to east across the coast and dominate the Washington coast; this weather system causes considerable day-to-day variations in wind speed and direction and can produce hurricane-like conditions. The counterclockwise winds associated with cyclonic activity predominantly originate from the south and southeast near the Washington coast.

Wave

Figures to the right shows wave rose and joint plots between significant wave height  (Hs) and peak wave period (Tp). Wave rose shows that the predominant wave direction is from west to northwest, while the extreme waves are generally from the west. The joint plot indicates that most wave energy is concentrated around 2 m heights with 10 sec periods. It is important to understand how much wave energy associated with such conditions can reach into the harbors, given the moderate wave periods. The extremely long wave periods above 18 sec rarely coincide with very large wave heights.

Hydrodynamic-Validation

A dynamically coupled hydrodynamic, wave, and sediment transport model was developed using Delft3D‑FM to understand the hydro- and morpho-dynamics within the Twin Harbors as well as to determine the effects of dredging and disposal activities in Grays Harbor on the fate of the dredged sediments.

The model domain, extends roughly 450 km offshore and 200 km north and south of Grays Harbor and Willapa Bay. Spatial resolution ranges from 50 km offshore to 90-120 m in the tidal flats and 30-60 m in the channels. Hydrodynamics are driven by tidal constituents at the offshore boundaries from the ADCIRC tidal database, discharge into the domain from major rivers in the Twin Harbors, and temporally and spatially varying wind and pressure fields from the NCEP CFSR dataset.
Parameters within the model, such as bottom friction and eddy viscosity and diffusivity, were calibrated and validated using a field survey of water level, current, wave height along the entrance of Grays Harbor in 1999 by USACE. The model skill was evaluated using Taylor's Diagram and measured by a score ranging from 0 (worst) to 1 (best). The model skill is greater than 0.9 with a mean close to 0.95 for water level, and greater than 0.9 for currents except one location, and greater than 0.8 for wave height in general.

Hydrodynamic-Circulation

The hydrodynamics are evaluated using the residual current over a 1-month period. In general, a net outward flow through the channel causes localized circulation patterns at the entrance and within the inlet, which feed the water to the deeper part of the channel. Circulation patterns north of the inlet entrance potentially send sediments exiting the harbor back towards Ocean Shores due to the presence of a stronger ebb current. Additionally, the circulation pattern within the inlet indicates a potential sediment pathway from North Jetty to Damon Point, and an eastward sediment pathway within Half Moon Bay.
